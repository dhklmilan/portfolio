Community Forestry Works

2020 – Ongoing · Community Engagement

Community forestry is all about people and their forests—working together to care for the land while ensuring it meets their needs. Since starting my Bachelor of Science in Forestry, I’ve been diving into this field, from trekking through forests to collect data to sitting down with communities to draft operational plans and constitutions. Honestly, the best part of this work has been connecting with people in so many different places. I spent a month in Khotang district, where I saw how locals manage their forests. Then there was Okhaldhunga and Solukhumbu—mountain regions, where village has its own way of working with the forested land. Down in the lower regions, places like Makawanpur, Bara, and Chitwan taught me something entirely different. Here, I got involved in both traditional community forestry and scientific approaches. It’s fascinating to see how these two methods can come together to address real challenges. These days, I’m working on creating operational plans for forests in central Nepal, especially in Chitwan and Nawalpur. In Nepal, community forestry isn’t just a program—it’s a way of life. When forests are managed this way, they’re not just resources—they’re part of the community, part of the future.

Sustainable Forestry, Community Empowerment, Conservation, Forest Stewardship

Research Intern

Forest Research and Training Centre (FRTC)

Feb 2023 - July 2023 · 6 months
Kathmandu, Nepal

I completed a six-month research internship at the Forest Research and Training Centre in Kathmandu, Nepal. This experience provided me with valuable experience in a range of areas, including:

• Developing allometric equations for seven major species, encompassing fieldwork, lab work, data entry, and data cleansing.
• Assessing bamboo resources outside forest areas using visual image interpretation via Collect Earth Online (CEO), conducting laboratory work for biomass modeling, and implementing Quality Assurance and Quality Control (QA/QC) processes.
• Conducting LiDAR-based scanning field surveys in western Nepal.
• Applying research methodologies for forest research and data analytics.
• Performing Land Use Land Cover Analysis, carbon mapping, forest degradation and restoration mapping for carbon trade.

During my internship, I focused specifically on forest fire risk modeling in the Chure Terai Madhesh Landscape (CTML), a vulnerable area in Nepal. This work gave me valuable insights into forest fire research and allowed me to contribute to understanding and mitigating forest fire risks. I also collaborated with a team of experts, gathering their input and analyzing data related to forest fire incidents, climate patterns, vegetation types, and other environmental factors. I primarily focused my internship on using advanced statistical and geographic information system (GIS) tools to develop accurate forest fire risk models. Through this experience, I gained valuable skills in data analysis, GIS applications, research methodologies, and collaborating effectively with various stakeholders.

Data Analysis, GIS Mapping, Forest Fire Risk, Environmental Research

Digital Soil Mapping in Nepal

National Soil Science Research Center (NARC)
Digital Soil Map of Nepal

Feb 18th – March 3rd, 2021

I had the privilege of being part of a soil survey and sampling project for Digital Soil Mapping in the Makawanpur district, Nepal, as a field crew member.

This initiative was led by the International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT) in collaboration with the National Soil Science Research Center (NSSRC) under Nepal Agricultural Research Council (NARC). The field work involved soil sample collection, classification, and analysis, focusing on both physical and chemical properties. On the physical side, characteristics like horizons, depth, boundaries, color, texture, structure, and porosity were observed. For chemical analysis, soil pH and organic matter content were observed in field (pit).

What made this project particularly interesting was exploring how soil types varied across Nepal’s distinct physiographic zones, from the Terai and Chure to the Mid-hills. In total, soil samples were collected from 56 districts, covering all seven provinces of Nepal. These samples were then combined with environmental covariates—data derived from satellite imagery—to provide a more comprehensive picture. Later, for spatial predictions of soil properties at a resolution of 250 meters machine learning algorithms were used.

The results of this project were hosted on a digital platform by NARC, creating a national soil information system for Nepal. This system is designed to be periodically updated, ensuring it remains a valuable resource for both local and provincial stakeholders. Beyond the technical achievements, this effort has raised awareness about soil management and its importance at all levels.

Soil Composition, Land Use, Environmental Management, Agriculture
